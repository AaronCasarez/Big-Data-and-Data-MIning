#Problem Description
  #The housing market in King County is rapidly evolving, posing challenges for buyers and investors. Jacob Kawalski seeks to leverage data-driven insights to predict home prices effectively, helping his real estate business thrive in this competitive landscape.

#Objective
# The objective is to explore the dataset, identify key predictors of housing prices, and build a robust predictive model using data mining techniques. This will allow accurate price estimation for new properties.

#Transformations and Pre-Processing
  # A little less than the previous project, this involved reordering the variables so that both of our models only looked at a specific set of variables that we desired. These variables are: 8: bedrooms (Number of bedrooms in the house), 9: bathrooms (Number of bathrooms in the house), 10: sqft_living (Total square footage of living area), 12: floors (Number of floors in the house), 13: waterfront (Indicator of whether the property has waterfront access), 14: view (Quality of the view from the property), 15: condition (Overall condition of the house), 16: grade (Quality of construction and design, on a scale), 19: yr_built (Year the house was built), and 7: price (Target variable: The sale price of the house).

#Regression analysis:
  # The regression model demonstrates strong overall significance and predictive capacity. The F-statistic of 2300 and a p-value below 2.2e-16 confirm that the predictors collectively contribute significantly to explaining housing prices. All individual predictors are also statistically significant at the 0.001 level, highlighting their importance in the model. Key variables such as sqft_living, grade, and waterfront show the largest impact on price, with higher square footage, better design quality, and waterfront access creating important increases. Also, an increase in the number of bedrooms is associated with a price decrease, which might indicate that overall size (sqft_living) is a more critical factor than room count. The RMSE for the training set of 215,681 and validation set of 224,344 are both lower than the standard deviation of the housing prices, indicating that the model provides relatively accurate predictions. Additionally, all predictors have a Variance Inflation Factor below 10, suggesting low multicollinearity and a stable model. 

#Decision Tree
  #The RMSE of the training set is 93,116.43, indicating that the model fits the training data very well. However, the RMSE for the validation set increases to 183,953. This discrepancy where the error in the validation set is higher than in the training set is a fairly common characteristic of a good model, as it demonstrates that the decision tree generalizes reasonably well rather than overfitting the training data.

# Best Model Selection
  #Compared to the regression model, the decision tree exhibits a substantially lower RMSE for the validation set of 183,953 versus over 200,000. This reduction in error suggests that the decision tree is a better predictor of housing prices, capturing complex relationships in the data that the regression model might not fully address. The ability to reduce error in out-of-sample predictions is a critical strength, particularly for practical applications like estimating housing prices for unseen properties. Due to these reasons, we chose this model to analyze the new data given.
